How Does the Biological Clock Tick?
A passage about ageing, metabolism and the limits of life span

AOur life span is restricted. Everyone accepts this as biologically obvious: nothing lives for ever. However, in making this statement we often think first of technical objects that are subjected to natural wear and tear during use. When this happens, the object gradually stops working and eventually becomes unusable. For living organisms, though, this is not a sufficient explanation of ageing. At first sight we may believe that we grow old because our bodies wear out. But if that were the whole story, it ought to be possible to replace worn-out parts and remain forever young. Clearly, that is not the case.

BOur dead products are static, closed systems. As they are built, no matter which materials they are made from, it is always the basic material which becomes worn down and older. In this sense, ageing follows the laws of physical chemistry and thermodynamics. Living organisms are different. They are open systems, fed from outside and maintained in dynamic equilibrium. It is not the basic material that matters most, but the functional whole. Wear and tear may contribute to decline, but it cannot be the decisive factor. Within seven years, about 90 per cent of the material of the human body is replaced as new, yet the organism maintains its form and continues to function.

CThus ageing and death should not be seen as inevitable, particularly as organisms possess many mechanisms for repair. In principle, a biological system could become older without ageing. Nevertheless, a limited life span, followed by ageing and death, is a basic characteristic of life. The reason is easy to see: in nature, living things either adapt or are regularly replaced by new types. Because changes in genetic material create new characteristics, individual organisms are tested for better adaptation to environmental conditions. Immortality would disturb this system because evolution needs room for the appearance of new and better life.

DEvery organism has a life span that is highly characteristic. There are striking differences between species, but within one species the parameter is relatively constant. For example, the average duration of human life has hardly changed over thousands of years. Medical care and improved nutrition have allowed more people to reach advanced age, yet the characteristic upper limit for most people remains about 80 years. Another argument against a simple wear-and-tear theory is that the time within which organisms age can vary enormously, from a few days or even hours for unicellular organisms to several thousand years for giant trees.

EIf a life span is a genetically determined biological characteristic, it is logically necessary to propose the existence of an internal clock, which in some way measures and controls the ageing process and finally determines death as the last step in a fixed programme. Like life span, metabolic rate has for different organisms a fixed mathematical relationship to body mass. In comparison with life span, this relationship is inverted: the larger the organism, the lower its metabolic rate. This pattern is valid not only for birds, but broadly for plants, animals and even unicellular organisms.

FAnimals which behave frugally with energy often become particularly old, for example crocodiles and tortoises. Parrots and birds of prey are often kept chained, so they are not able to experience life as actively and therefore attain a high life span in captivity. Animals which save energy by hibernation or lethargy, such as bats or hedgehogs, live much longer than those which are always active. The same pattern appears in human beings: women become distinctly older than men, and the higher male metabolic rate roughly accounts for the lower male life span. Men live more energetically, but not for as long.

GIt follows from the above that sparing use of energy reserves should tend to extend life. Extreme high-performance sports may produce excellent cardiovascular fitness, but they certainly do not prolong life. Relaxation lowers metabolic rate, as does adequate sleep and, in general, an equable and balanced personality. Each of us can therefore develop an energy-saving programme with a little self-observation, critical self-control and logical consistency. The point is not inactivity, but a wiser use of the body's resources.
