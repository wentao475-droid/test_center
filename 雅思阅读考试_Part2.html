Collecting Ant Specimens
A practical overview of why ants are collected and the main field methods used

ACollecting ants can be as simple as picking up stray individuals and placing them in a glass jar, or as complicated as completing an exhaustive survey of all species present in an area and estimating their relative abundance. The exact method used will depend on the final purpose of the collection. For taxonomy, or classification, long series from a single nest are preferred because they contain the different castes and allow researchers to study variation within one species. For ecological studies, by contrast, the most important factor is obtaining identifiable samples of as many different species as possible. Unfortunately, those two goals are not always compatible.

BTo collect as wide a range of species as possible, several methods must be used. These include hand collecting, using baits to attract ants, sampling ground litter, and the use of pitfall traps. Hand collecting consists of searching wherever ants are likely to occur: on the ground, under stones or logs, in rotting wood, in vegetation, on trunks and beneath bark. When possible, specimens should be taken from nests or foraging columns, and at least 20 to 25 individuals should be collected so that all members of the sample belong to the same species and can be used for detailed study.

CSince some species are largely nocturnal, collecting should not be confined to daytime. Ants can be gathered using an aspirator, forceps, a fine moistened paint brush, or even fingers if the species is harmless. Individual insects are usually placed in plastic or glass tubes containing 75% to 95% ethanol. Plastic tubes with secure tops are often preferred because they are lighter and less likely to break. The resulting material is especially useful for taxonomic investigations, where separate specimens may need to be compared in detail.

DBaits can also be used to attract ants. A small amount of food is placed on the ground, on a tree trunk, on low vegetation, or close to a nest entrance and then checked regularly. This method is particularly useful for species that are hard to find because a bait can draw workers out from hidden nests. However, baiting does not usually give a balanced picture of all the species living in an area, because only those attracted to the chosen food will be sampled.

EMany ants are small and forage primarily in the layer of leaves and other debris on the ground. Collecting these species by hand can be difficult. One of the most successful approaches is to gather the leaf litter in which they are foraging and extract the ants from it. This is commonly done by placing the litter on a screen above a large funnel, often under some heat. As the litter dries from above, ants and other small animals move downward and eventually fall through the funnel into alcohol placed below. This method works especially well in rain forests and marshy areas. The catch can be improved by sifting the litter through a coarse screen first, which removes larger leaves and twigs and allows more litter to be sampled with a limited number of funnels.

FThe pitfall trap is another commonly used tool. A pitfall trap can be any small container placed in the ground with the top level with the surrounding surface and filled with a preservative. Ants are collected when they fall into the trap while foraging. The diameter of traps can vary considerably, and the number used may range from a few to several hundred depending on the aims of the study and the time available. Larger traps are generally more effective, but the collector must balance efficiency against convenience. Preservatives such as ethylene glycol or alcohol are common, but formalin should be avoided if the material may later be used for taxonomic work.
